About my teaching

The heart of my teaching has always been helping students discover their unrealized potential, whether they have interests in the substance of my discipline or not. Most of my classroom teaching involves helping students understand how political science's central findings and statistical methods can be used not only to better comprehend the political world as a whole but also to critically examine their own political views and those of people with whom they disagree. While there are lots of ways to teach political science effectively, I fall squarely in the faculty camp that feels comfortable teaching what some folks call "divisive" concepts. Thus, I don't avoid controversial topics or hide my own views on them, and I encourage students with all kinds of different views to engage in lively classroom discussions as their authentic selves. For me, a political science professor's core obligation isn't to convince students to adopt particular partisan or policy positions but rather to equip them with the knowledge, facts, and social-scientific tools to think more rigorously about politics for themselves.

While I have published continuously during my career, effective skills-building, mentoring, and student placement have been my highest priority. My former advisees have pursued successful careers in a huge range of fields, including positions with the State Department, on Capitol Hill, in the federal government, in the Armed Forces and national security agencies, in banking and finance, as college and high school teachers, as clergy, in state governments, and with advocacy groups as ideologically diverse as the Heritage Foundation and Emily's List.
